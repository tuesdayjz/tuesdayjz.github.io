Now that I've finished graduate school and I guess I won't be back to school for many years, I want to take some time to reflect on what I experienced in my school life and put my thoughts/feelings into words.
I planned to write this by the end of March but between getting ready for a new life, the days slipped by...

## While in Kindergarten

I was born at a hospital in Kawasaki, Kanagawa, but until I was old enough to start preschool, I was sent to Shanghai to live with my grandparents so my mother could properly care for my older sister. I don't remember anything of that time, but after growing up, my mother often said that my tendency to get cavities was because they had given me so many candies back then.

Not long after I started at a Japanese nursery school and learned to read, I became an bookworm. By around three years old, I could read picture books by myself, and from there, I started reading every single character I saw -- children's books and novels, of course, and newspapers, ingredient labels, receipts, warning statement... anything with words on it.

I love writing just as much. I remember filling page after page of a sketchbook my mother bought for me with original stories instead of drawings, much to my parents' disappointment. I also enjoyed doing math workbooks, and had mastered basic arithmetic well before I started my elementary school education.

## Elementary School

I have been bad at making friends since then. I'm not sure why...

In second grade, a close friend I'd had since nursery school moved away and transferred to another school — and after that, life at school became noticeably harder. I couldn't find much common ground with my classmates, the pace of lessons felt slow and dull, and I found myself heading to the library every single break.

Starting in fourth grade, I began attending a cram school, and that changed things. There was always something fascinating to learn, and if I arrived early, I could work on puzzles or be quizzed by the teachers. For the first time, I had adults to talk to outside of school teachers and my parents, and little by little, my days started to feel more enjoyable. I never quite fit in with the brass band club, but cram school was the one thing I genuinely looked forward to each day.

From fifth grade, I joined the broadcasting committee at school. It meant I could bring my lunch into the broadcast room and spend time there, and I liked it a lot. Many of the other members shared a similar background to mine and had their own moments of feeling out of place at school, so we got along well. I also loved being in that cramped little room packed with broadcasting equipment. Best of all, the library was right across from the broadcast room, so even when it wasn't my turn on duty, I'd often bring a book in and read.

Around that time, a large apartment complex was built near the school, and a wave of transfer students arrived. I found that I had a lot more in common with them, and those final two years turned out to be the best of my entire elementary school life.
